Training Priorities — Ranked by Impact

Five areas ordered by expected time-drop potential. Each has a concrete target, practice sets, and a success metric.

Priority 1 — Highest Impact

Back-Half Conditioning

~1.5–2.0 sec potential

The split data makes this concrete: a ~6-second front-to-back gap, where AA-level swimmers typically show 2.5–3.5 seconds. Closing this gap is both the biggest opportunity and the most directly coachable one. Coach Jasper is already aware and working this area.

Target: Back 50 within 4 seconds of front 50 under race conditions (from ~6 sec gap today to ≤4 sec).

Practice Sets
  • Race-pace repeat 50s (6–8 × 50 @ 100-race pace, 30 sec rest) — train the body to sustain speed after the turn.
  • Descending 75s (3–4 × 75 where the last 25 must match first 25 pace) — simulate the critical 50–75m zone.
  • Over-distance threshold sets (4 × 150 @ slightly below race pace) — build aerobic base for the back half.
  • Kick 50s targeting the back half only — isolate the legs where they matter most.
Success metric: Front/back split gap decreases from ~6 sec to ≤4 sec within 6 weeks of focused work. Record split on every timed 100 free effort.
Priority 2

Turns & Underwater Kicking

~0.5–0.8 sec

This is the single biggest differentiator between A and AA swimmers. Elite 15-year-olds hold 5–7 meters of powerful underwater kick off every turn. In the 100 free there are 3 turns — 0.5m gained per turn compounds quickly. The core and leg dryland work already in the program directly supports this. Underwater distances haven’t been measured yet — getting a baseline this week is step one.

Target: Consistent 5–6 m underwaters off all three turns in the 100 free (measure weekly).

Practice Sets
  • Mark the 5m and 6m flags on the lane rope — must visually pass the 5m mark before surfacing on every turn, every practice.
  • Underwater kick sets: 6 × 25 underwater only (streamline off wall, flutter or dolphin kick to the flag, then freestyle to wall).
  • Turn mechanics check: approach speed, flip timing, toe-plant position, rotation tightness — film from the side wall monthly.
  • 10 push-offs per practice holding the tightest possible streamline — builds muscle memory quickly.
Success metric: Measured underwater distance ≥5m off all 3 turns on 3 consecutive timed 100 frees. Action item: measure baseline this week.
Priority 3

Race Execution & Consistency

~0.3–0.6 sec

The split data shows Mya’s front-50 pacing is already solid — she’s not going out too hard. The focus here is execution consistency under pressure: can she hit the same controlled front 50 every time she races, regardless of nerves or competition?

Target: Execute assigned splits within 0.3 seconds in practice 100s, consistently.

Practice Sets
  • Time every 100 free effort in practice with split data — make the front/back gap visible and trackable on a chart.
  • Race-simulation 100s with explicit split targets assigned beforehand — test the ability to execute under pressure.
  • Mental rehearsal: identify the 40m mark as the “commitment point” — hold form and drive hard through to the finish from there.
  • Review splits with Coach Jasper weekly — data-driven conversation about what’s working.
Success metric: Consistent split execution within 0.3 sec of target in practice 100s for 3 consecutive sessions.
Priority 4

Start & Block Reaction

~0.3–0.5 sec in 50 free

The start accounts for roughly 20–25% of the 50 free. A clean entry angle, tight streamline, and powerful underwater off the block are quickly coachable and hold immediately in meets. This is especially high-value in the 50 free.

Target: Reaction time ≤0.65 sec; underwater distance off start ≥7m.

Practice Sets
  • 2–3 start reps per practice from the block — non-negotiable daily habit.
  • Focus sequence: load → hip hinge → drive angle → arm sweep → tuck → kick sequence. Drill it the same way every time.
  • Time the reaction: use a starter’s beep app or coach with stopwatch from beep to water entry.
  • Underwater 25s off the start: sprint to the 25m wall using only start underwaters + minimal strokes — isolates start efficiency.
Success metric: Measurable improvement in underwater distance off the start vs. current baseline within 4 weeks.
Priority 5

Breathing Pattern in the 50 Free

~0.3–0.5 sec

Mya currently breathes 2–3 times in the 50 free. Each breath costs time through head turn, body rotation, and drag. Many elite 50 freestylers take 0–1 breaths. Going from 2–3 to 1 or 0 is a structured, testable experiment that can yield a quick win — potentially one of the fastest gains on this list relative to effort required.

Target: Identify Mya’s optimal breath count through a 3-week structured test.

3-Week Breathing Test
  • Week 1: Timed 50s with current pattern (2–3 breaths) → establish a clean baseline.
  • Week 2: Timed 50s with 0 breaths → compare total time.
  • Week 3: Timed 50s with 1 breath at the 25m turn → compare.
  • Select the fastest reliable pattern and drill it as the permanent standard.
  • If going no-breath: build CO₂ tolerance via hypoxic sets (e.g., 8 × 50 breathing every 5 strokes).
Success metric: Optimal breath count identified and used consistently in all 50 free efforts within 3 weeks. Expectation: 0.2–0.4 sec improvement in 50 free from breath reduction alone.

Project Rules

  • Data first. Every training decision ties back to a number — split times, underwater distances, reaction times. No guessing.
  • Prioritize by ROI. Work from the low-hanging fruit list in ranked order. Don’t chase marginal gains before the big ones are captured.
  • Coachability over volume. Technique and race-execution work beat simply swimming more yards. Work with Coach Jasper, not around.
  • Split every 100. Mya should know her first-50 vs second-50 split from every timed 100 free effort. The split is the primary diagnostic tool.
  • Measure turns. Track underwater distance off every turn (target: 5–7m) at least once per week. Small gains compound.
  • Breathing is a variable. Systematically test breath counts in the 50 free. Currently 2–3 — never leave it as habit.
  • Peak at the right meet. Identify the target A-meet and build the training cycle backward from that date. (Target meet TBD — needed to build the cycle.)
  • Weekly check-in. One short review per week: what was tested, what improved, what’s next.

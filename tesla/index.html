Trim Recommendation

Speed, AWD traction, and extra range are all things your parents will never use. Here's exactly what each trim means and why two of the four are easy to skip.

First: What "Dual Motor" and "AWD" Actually Mean

Every Tesla has electric motors — the question is how many and where.

⚡

Single Motor — RWD

One motor at the rear axle. Power goes to the back two wheels only. Simple, efficient, and the reason RWD trims have more range — less hardware, less energy loss. This is what Standard and Long Range RWD use.

⚡⚡

Dual Motor — AWD

One motor at the front axle, one at the rear — all-wheel drive. Better traction on wet or slippery roads, noticeably faster acceleration. Slightly lower range because you're powering more hardware. This is what AWD and Long Range AWD use.

In a gas car, AWD means complex driveshafts and transfer cases. In a Tesla it's just adding a second electric motor — which is why the price jump is relatively modest. But for Austin, TX in mostly dry weather, you likely won't feel the difference day-to-day.

On the Hardware Obsolescence Concern

This is a legitimate concern and worth taking seriously — Tesla has already done one hardware cycle (HW3 → HW4). Here's the current state of play as of March 2026:

✅

What You Get Today: HW4 (Confirmed)

All 2026 Model Ys ship with Hardware 4 (HW4). In late 2025 some units briefly showed a label reading "HW4.5" — Tesla clarified this was a documentation mistake. There is no new FSD computer in current Model Y deliveries. HW4 runs FSD V14 and is actively receiving updates.

🔮

What's Coming: HW5 (AI5) — But Not Yet

HW5 is real — roughly 4–5× more compute than HW4. Elon confirmed in January 2026 that mass production has been pushed back to mid-2027, with a small number of early units going to the Cybercab (robotaxi) first, not the Model Y. Standard Model Y buyers are realistically looking at 2027–2028 before HW5 is widely available.

🏗️

HW4's Track Record: Long-Supported Hardware

HW3 cars from 2019–2022 are still receiving FSD updates in 2026 — Tesla just announced FSD V14 Lite for HW3 in Q2 2026. That's a 5–6 year runway for the previous generation. HW4 is far more capable, suggesting a similarly long support window.

💡

The Low-Mileage Advantage

At 6,000 miles per year, a purchased Model Y will have roughly 18,000–24,000 miles in 3–4 years — nearly new by any standard. If and when HW4 becomes a meaningful ceiling, you'll be selling a low-mileage vehicle that commands a strong resale price, effectively funding any future upgrade.

⚠️ The "Fully Unsupervised" Scenario — What It Changes

This is the sharpest version of the hardware concern: What if HW5 doesn't just offer better FSD, but eliminates the need for driver supervision entirely?

If that happens, today's HW4 car and a future HW5 car aren't just different generations — they're categorically different products. HW4 + FSD is a driver-assistance system that requires Dad to stay alert and supervise. A truly driverless car removes that requirement entirely. For aging parents, that distinction is enormous.

This scenario makes the case for leasing stronger — a 2–3 year lease would position you to upgrade into an HW5 car without the friction of selling and rebuying. At the same time, it also raises a third option worth considering: wait a bit longer before buying at all.

But This Scenario Has Three Independent Hurdles

1. Hardware: HW5 must ship in volume in the Model Y — mid-2027 at earliest for mass production.

2. Software: FSD must reach unsupervised-quality maturity on HW5. Tesla has been "almost there" on full autonomy for years — hardware alone doesn't guarantee software readiness.

3. Regulation: Federal and Texas regulators must approve unsupervised Level 4 driving in personal vehicles. This is the most unpredictable variable and is entirely outside Tesla's control. Timeline: unknown.


All three hurdles need to clear simultaneously. The realistic window for a fully unsupervised Model Y you can just buy off the lot is 2028–2030 at the earliest — not 2027.

The Bottom Line

The hardware obsolescence argument for leasing is more real than it was a year ago, and shouldn't be dismissed. HW5 is closer than expected. The question to ask: how much is the option to refresh hardware worth to you in dollars? Leasing will cost more overall. Is that premium worth the peace of mind?

For Mom and Dad's situation — under 6,000 miles/year, need help now, Marcus nearby to manage — buying still wins on the math. HW4 will be well-supported for years, and the low-mileage resale position is a natural upgrade path. But if fully unsupervised driving feels like the real goal and waiting a year is feasible, that's a reasonable choice too. The worst outcome is rushing into a purchase today because of urgency, only to watch HW5 arrive 12 months later.
